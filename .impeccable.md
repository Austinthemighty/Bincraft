## Design Context

### Users
**Dual audience**: Shop floor operators scanning Kanban cards on tablets/phones between tasks, and ops/inventory managers at desks tracking stock levels, creating purchase orders, and reviewing analytics on laptops. Both groups use the same system, but with different primary workflows — operators scan and pull, managers plan and analyze.

**Context**: Manufacturing environments. Operators are often standing, moving between workstations, scanning quickly. Managers sit at desks reviewing data in longer sessions. The system needs to serve both: fast physical interactions AND deep analytical views.

### Brand Personality
**Efficient. Reliable. No-nonsense.**

This is a tool, not a toy. It should feel like precision equipment — something that earns trust by being consistently dependable and getting out of the way. No decoration for decoration's sake. Every element exists because it helps someone do their job faster or with more confidence.

The emotional goal is *quiet competence*: the interface should feel like it already knows what you need next.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Theme**: Dark mode — managers spend long sessions reviewing data, and a dark theme reduces fatigue. Operators on the floor benefit from high-contrast elements on dark backgrounds.
- **Primary color**: Blue retained as the anchor color, but refined — not the default Tailwind blue. A distinctive blue that feels purposeful and industrial.
- **Visual tone**: Dense and information-rich where needed (dashboards, tables), spacious and focused where speed matters (scanning, quick actions). Not minimal for the sake of minimal — functional density.
- **Anti-references**: Should NOT look like generic SaaS dashboards, Tailwind UI templates, or admin panels. Should NOT look "startup-y" or playful. Should feel purpose-built for manufacturing, not adapted from a CRM.

### Design Principles

1. **Speed over aesthetics**: If a design choice slows a workflow by even one tap or one second of confusion, reject it. The fastest path wins.

2. **Status at a glance**: A user should understand the state of their inventory, orders, and cards within 2 seconds of looking at any screen. Color, position, and density communicate status — not labels.

3. **Scannable hierarchy**: Information is layered — most critical data is largest and highest contrast. Supporting details are present but recede. Nothing competes for attention with the primary action.

4. **Built for the job**: Every design decision should feel like it was made by someone who understands manufacturing floors, not someone who designs social media apps. Practical over pretty.

5. **Trust through consistency**: Same patterns, same positions, same behaviors across every page. Once a user learns one screen, they've learned the language of all screens.
